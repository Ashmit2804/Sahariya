Geography

Geography of the Study

The map locates villages and regions of Baran district, Rajasthan, where fieldwork has been conducted — a landscape that is inseparable from Sahariya cultural practice.

Fieldwork Area

Baran District, Rajasthan

Baran is a district in southeastern Rajasthan, bordering Madhya Pradesh. It is characterised by the Chambal river basin, substantial forest cover, and a diverse tribal population. The Sahariya community is concentrated in the Shahabad and Kishanganj blocks — areas that retain significant forest cover and have maintained distinct cultural practices.

The geographical context is essential to understanding Sahariya culture. Art practices, ecological knowledge, and ritual calendars are all intimately tied to the landscape — its seasons, its plants, its animals, and its rivers. Documentation of cultural practices without geographical documentation would be incomplete.

Map: Baran District, Rajasthan — primary fieldwork region. Key areas: Shahabad block (northwest), Kishanganj block (northeast). Base map © OpenStreetMap contributors.

Shahabad

24.92°N, 76.55°E

Primary fieldwork block. Dense forest cover. Strong Mandana painting tradition. Site of most visual documentation and elder interviews. Home to Kamla Bai and the women's art collective.

Kishanganj

24.80°N, 76.88°E

Second major fieldwork block. Active Swang performance tradition. Significant oral narrative documentation conducted here. Home to Prem Lal's performance troupe.

Baran Town

25.10°N, 76.51°E

District headquarters. Used as research base. Site of community workshops and review sessions. Several community members study or work here while maintaining village connections.

Forest Areas

Shahabad–Kishanganj belt

The remaining forest tracts between the two blocks are ecologically and culturally significant — sources of art materials (ochre, plant dyes), sites of seasonal gatherings, and subject of ecological narratives in songs and Swang.

Chambal River Zone

Eastern Baran

The Chambal and its tributaries appear prominently in Sahariya oral tradition. The river is a recurring motif in songs, a source of clay for ritual purposes, and a boundary-marker in community geography.

Festival Sites

Multiple locations

Seasonal melas and festivals take place at specific sacred sites across the region. These locations — often at forest clearings, river confluences, or hilltops — are the primary contexts for Swang performance and collective ritual Mandana.
