Research & Methodology

How We Work

An interdisciplinary methodology combining visual ethnography, performance studies, and participatory research — conducted over multiple seasons in Baran district.

Project Overview

Research Approach

This project is based on an interdisciplinary methodology combining visual ethnography, performance studies, and participatory research. It is situated at the intersection of art history, anthropology, and ecological studies — a location that allows it to address Sahariya cultural practices in their full complexity.

The research does not treat Sahariya art as a fixed object to be described and archived. Instead, it approaches art practices as dynamic processes — ongoing negotiations between tradition and change, between individual creativity and collective knowledge, between local meaning and global context.

Fieldwork

In the Field

Fieldwork conducted in the Baran district spans multiple seasons and involves sustained engagement with communities in Shahabad and Kishanganj blocks. The research is not extractive — each visit involves reciprocal exchange, community review, and collaborative decision-making about what is documented and how.

The fieldwork approach prioritises relationship over data collection. Researchers spend extended periods in communities, participating in daily life and cultural events rather than simply arriving to record specific practices. This approach, while slower, produces richer and more ethically grounded documentation.

Community workshops have been a central part of the methodology — occasions where community members review documentation materials, correct errors, add context, and decide what should and should not be included in the public archive.

Analytical Framework

Theoretical Foundations

The analytical framework integrates cultural theory, semiotics, and ecological perspectives to interpret Sahariya art as a living epistemology — a system of knowing expressed through practice rather than text. This framing draws on work in more-than-human geography, indigenous knowledge studies, and performance theory.

A key theoretical commitment is to treat the Sahariya not as a "primitive" or "pre-modern" society but as a sophisticated community with a complex knowledge system that is fully contemporary — engaged with the pressures and possibilities of the present moment while drawing on deep historical roots.

Visual Ethnography

Photography, video, and drawing as primary research methods, not just illustration.

Performance Studies

Treating cultural events as performances with structure, meaning, and agency.

Participatory Action Research

Community members as co-researchers, not subjects of study.

Ecological Anthropology

Understanding culture as embedded in and expressive of environmental relationships.

Cultural Semiotics

Reading visual and performative forms as complex sign systems.

Indigenous Knowledge Studies

Recognising non-Western knowledge systems as legitimate and sophisticated.

Research Ethics

The ethical dimensions of this research are not an afterthought — they are constitutive of the entire project. Working with a Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Group requires particular care and ongoing reflection.

Informed Consent

All documentation is conducted with explicit community consent. Materials are reviewed and approved before archiving.

Community Ownership

The community retains ownership of their cultural knowledge and images. The archive serves as a platform, not a claim.

Access Control

Some materials are restricted at community request. Sacred content is excluded from the public archive entirely.

Benefit Sharing

Research outputs are shared with the community in accessible formats, and community members receive recognition and compensation for their participation.
