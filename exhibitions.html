Exhibitions

Curated Collections

Thematic exhibitions that bring together selected materials from the archive — interpretive pathways for engaging with Sahariya culture beyond individual entries.

Four Themes

Explore the Exhibitions

Each exhibition is a curated selection of archive materials organised around a central interpretive theme. They are designed to offer multiple entry points into the archive — whether you come as an art lover, a researcher, an ecologist, or simply someone curious about Sahariya culture.

01

Exhibition 01

Art & Ecology

This exhibition brings together Mandana paintings, oral narratives, and field documentation to explore a central question: how does Sahariya art encode ecological knowledge?

The Mandana patterns in this exhibition are examined not merely as decorative objects but as repositories of information about landscape, season, plant life, and animal behaviour. Alongside the visual works, audio recordings of ecological songs and interviews with women artists discuss the specific plants, pigments, and seasonal rhythms that structure the art-making calendar.

The exhibition argues that for the Sahariya, ecological knowledge is inseparable from aesthetic knowledge — to know how to paint is to know how the world works.

02

Exhibition 02

Women & Mandana

Mandana is overwhelmingly a women's art form. This exhibition centres the women of the Sahariya community as artists, knowledge holders, and cultural transmitters — exploring the relationship between gender, creativity, and cultural continuity.

The exhibition includes portraits and extended testimonies from practitioners across three generations, as well as documentation of the social contexts in which Mandana is taught and learned. It examines how women's authority over this visual tradition gives them a form of cultural power that is rarely acknowledged in conventional accounts of tribal life.

Featured voices include Kamla Bai, Sunita, and Meena Bai — three generations whose conversations about Mandana illuminate both the continuity and the evolution of the tradition.

Women's Portraits Testimonies Generational Documentation Teaching Sessions
03

Exhibition 03

Ritual & Performance

This exhibition documents the ritual and performative life of the Sahariya community — the festivals, ceremonies, and theatrical traditions that mark time and maintain community cohesion.

Drawing on video recordings, audio documentation, and ethnographic photography, the exhibition takes viewers through the performative dimensions of the Sahariya ritual calendar: harvest ceremonies, life cycle events, seasonal melas, and Swang theatre. It emphasises performance not as spectacle but as a form of collective action and meaning-making.

A section on Swang theatre includes recordings of three different performances with contextual notes from performers and audience members, allowing viewers to understand how meaning is made in this participatory form.

Swang Theatre Festival Documentation Ritual Photography Oral Accounts
04

Exhibition 04

Continuity & Change

The fourth exhibition addresses change — arguably the most important and least understood dimension of living cultural traditions. It challenges the common assumption that "authentic" indigenous culture is static, and documents how Sahariya practices are actively evolving.

Through paired documentation from different time periods, interviews across generations, and testimonies from community members who have engaged with urban life and returned, this exhibition maps the dynamic relationship between tradition and transformation.

The exhibition includes reflections from Arjun — the young community member whose perspective opens the community voices section — and presents his argument that change is not a threat to Sahariya culture but one of its defining characteristics.
