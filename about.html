About

Who are the Sahariya?

The only Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Group (PVTG) in Rajasthan — a community whose art, ecology, and spirituality are deeply interwoven.

Identity & Place

A Community Rooted in Forest and Land

The Sahariya are the only Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Group (PVTG) in Rajasthan, primarily residing in Baran district, particularly in Shahabad and Kishanganj blocks. Historically dependent on forest ecosystems, the community has developed a deeply interconnected system of life in which art, ecology, and spirituality coexist.

Their cultural practices—ranging from Mandana wall paintings to Swang performances—reflect a worldview grounded in balance between human life, nature, and the divine. These traditions are not merely aesthetic forms but constitute a living system of knowledge, memory, and identity.

Despite their rich cultural heritage, the Sahariya remain largely absent from digital and academic representation. This platform seeks to address that absence by documenting and sharing knowledge systems in genuine collaboration with the community itself.

Culture & Ecology

A Worldview of Balance

The Sahariya relationship with their environment is not merely utilitarian—it is cosmological. Forest, land, water, and sky are understood as alive with agency, and cultural practices are forms of ongoing conversation with these forces.

Mandana paintings are not decorative additions to domestic space—they are ritual communications. Applied on walls and floors during festivals, harvests, births, and marriages, they mark transitions and invite protection, fertility, and cosmic order.

Similarly, Swang theatre and oral song traditions encode ecological knowledge—information about seasons, plants, animal behaviour, and rainfall—in forms that can be transmitted across generations without written text.

Why This Archive Matters

Addressing a Representational Gap

India's tribal communities are among the most culturally sophisticated yet least digitally documented peoples on earth. For the Sahariya, this gap is especially pronounced: their practices are not widely exhibited, their languages have limited written records, and their art forms are not included in mainstream art historical canons.

This archive is conceived as a corrective—not a salvage project (the community's culture is alive, not disappearing) but a platform for contemporary documentation and future visibility. It is built with community consent and participation, ensuring that the Sahariya speak for themselves.

Our Commitment

This platform is a collaborative digital archive that documents and interprets Sahariya art and culture as a living, evolving system of knowledge—created with and alongside the community, not merely about it.
