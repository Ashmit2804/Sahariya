Introduction

A Community at the Heart of Culture

The Sahariya community of Rajasthan represents one of India's most culturally rich yet underrepresented Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs). This platform brings together visual, performative, and oral traditions through long-term ethnographic fieldwork and collaborative documentation.

This digital archive documents Mandana paintings, ritual practices, performances, and oral narratives—not as static artefacts, but as living expressions of ecological knowledge, spirituality, and community life.

About

Who are the Sahariya?

The Sahariya are the only Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Group (PVTG) in Rajasthan, primarily residing in Baran district, particularly in Shahabad and Kishanganj. Historically dependent on forest ecosystems, the community has developed a deeply interconnected system of life in which art, ecology, and spirituality coexist.

Their cultural practices—ranging from Mandana wall paintings to Swang performances—reflect a worldview grounded in balance between human life, nature, and the divine. These traditions are not merely aesthetic forms, but constitute a living system of knowledge, memory, and identity.

Despite their rich cultural heritage, the Sahariya remain largely absent from digital and academic representation. This platform seeks to address that absence by documenting and sharing its knowledge systems in collaboration with the community itself.

Visual Culture

Art as Living Knowledge

Mandana Paintings

Mandana are ritual wall and floor paintings created using natural materials such as clay, lime, and plant pigments. These geometric and symbolic designs are closely tied to festivals, agricultural cycles, and life events.

Materials & Techniques

Colours are derived from locally available materials—red ochre, white lime, black carbon, and plant-based dyes—reflecting a sustainable, ecological approach to art-making.

Symbolism & Meaning

Motifs often represent fertility, protection, harmony, and cosmic order. These visual forms function as a cultural language through which the community expresses its worldview.

Sahariya visual culture is embedded in everyday life, ritual practice, and ecological relationships. Created primarily by women, these art forms express cosmology, morality, and social structure.

Performative Traditions

Voice, Memory, and Movement

Sahariya culture is equally expressed through performance, oral storytelling, and ritual enactments. These traditions carry collective memory, ethical values, and ecological knowledge across generations.

Swang Theatre

A performative tradition combining drama, music, and storytelling. It reflects the community's social realities, mythological narratives, and moral teachings.

Folk Songs & Oral Narratives

Songs and stories serve as repositories of history, belief systems, and lived experiences—performed during rituals, festivals, and communal gatherings.

Ritual Performances

Local fairs and seasonal celebrations bring together performance, art, and community participation, reinforcing social bonds and cultural continuity.

Research

Research Approach

This project is based on an interdisciplinary methodology combining visual ethnography, performance studies, and participatory research.

Fieldwork conducted in the Baran district involves sustained community engagement over multiple seasons, building relationships of trust and reciprocity with Sahariya artists, elders, and youth.

The research adopts a collaborative approach, engaging Sahariya community members as co-researchers and knowledge holders. This ensures that documentation is not extractive but dialogic and ethically grounded.

The analytical framework integrates cultural theory, semiotics, and ecological perspectives to interpret Sahariya art as a living epistemology—a system of knowledge expressed through practice rather than text.

Fieldwork Methods

  • Participant observation
  • Semi-structured interviews
  • Audio-visual documentation
  • Community workshops
  • Collaborative archiving sessions
  • Ethical review & community consent

Research Framework

  • Visual ethnography
  • Performance studies
  • Participatory action research
  • Cultural semiotics
  • Ecological anthropology
